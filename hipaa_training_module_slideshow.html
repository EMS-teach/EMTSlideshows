What is HIPAA?

HIPAA was enacted in 1996 and provides for criminal sanctions as well as civil penalties for releasing a patient's confidential medical information in an unauthorized manner.

Key Components

  • Privacy Rule: Protects individually identifiable health information
  • Security Rule: Sets standards for protecting electronic PHI
  • Breach Notification Rule: Requires notification when PHI is compromised
HIPAA applies to EMS because: Most EMS agencies are "covered entities" that provide healthcare and transmit health information electronically (billing, patient records, etc.).

Protected Health Information (PHI)

PHI includes medical information or any information that can be used to identify the patient.

Examples of PHI Include:

  • Patient name, address, phone number
  • Date of birth, Social Security number
  • Medical record numbers
  • Account numbers
  • Photos, videos, or audio recordings
  • Biometric identifiers
  • Any health information that can identify an individual
EMS Scenario: You respond to a car accident. The patient's medical history, vital signs, treatments provided, and even the fact that they were transported to the hospital all constitute PHI.

Electronic PHI (ePHI)

Any PHI that is stored, accessed, or transmitted electronically, including:

  • Electronic patient care reports (ePCRs)
  • Digital photos or videos
  • Email communications about patients
  • Electronic medical records
Source: 45 CFR 164.501; Advanced Emergency Care and Transportation textbook; NEMSIS documentation standards

EMS and HIPAA Compliance

EMS providers must safeguard all PHI from unlawful disclosure, whether written or verbal.

Required Safeguards

  • Administrative: Policies, procedures, training programs
  • Physical: Securing devices, limiting access to workstations
  • Technical: Encryption, access controls, audit logs
Never leave electronic devices or patient reports containing PHI unattended! This includes tablets, laptops, paper PCRs, and mobile devices.

Special EMS Considerations

  • PHI must be maintained when multiple patients are in the ambulance
  • Avoid discussing patient information where others can overhear
  • Each EMS agency must have a designated privacy officer
  • Emergency situations don't exempt you from HIPAA requirements

Permitted Uses and Disclosures

HIPAA allows disclosure of PHI without patient authorization for Treatment, Payment, and Operations (TPO).

Treatment

  • Providing, coordinating, or managing healthcare
  • Consulting with other healthcare providers
  • Referring patients for healthcare
EMS Example: You can share patient information with the receiving hospital emergency department to ensure continuity of care.

Payment

  • Activities related to billing and collection
  • Insurance verification and claims processing
  • Utilization review activities

Healthcare Operations

  • Quality assurance and improvement activities
  • Training programs for healthcare professionals
  • Compliance with regulatory requirements

Other Permitted Disclosures

  • Required by law (court orders, subpoenas)
  • Public health activities
  • Law enforcement (limited circumstances)
  • To prevent serious threat to health or safety

Minimum Necessary Standard

Use, disclose, or request only the minimum amount of PHI necessary to accomplish the intended purpose.

Key Principles

  • Only access PHI needed for your specific job function
  • Share only relevant information with other healthcare providers
  • Limit who has access to patient information
  • Review and limit routine disclosures
EMS Scenario: When giving report to the hospital, share only relevant clinical information needed for patient care. Don't include unrelated personal details or family dynamics unless medically relevant.

Exceptions to Minimum Necessary

  • Disclosures to healthcare providers for treatment
  • Disclosures required by law
  • Uses or disclosures authorized by the patient
Remember: Even when minimum necessary doesn't apply, professional judgment should guide what information is shared.
Source: 45 CFR 164.502(b); HHS Minimum Necessary Guidance

Social Media and Technology Concerns

Any sharing of private or protected information, whether directly or verbally, is a serious violation of HIPAA and ethical principles.

Social Media Guidelines

  • Never post patient information on social media
  • No photos, videos, or audio of patients
  • Avoid identifiable information even if names are removed
  • Be professional in all online communications
Zero Tolerance: Posting patient information on social media can result in immediate termination and legal consequences.

Technology Best Practices

  • Use strong passwords and don't share login credentials
  • Log out of systems when finished
  • Encrypt sensitive communications
  • Report lost or stolen devices immediately
  • Only use agency-approved communication methods
Agency Ownership: Information recorded using agency equipment or during duty hours typically belongs to the agency and must follow strict retention and disclosure rules.

Breach Notification Requirements

A breach is an unauthorized acquisition, access, use, or disclosure of PHI that compromises its security or privacy.

Examples of Breaches

  • Lost or stolen devices containing PHI
  • Sending PHI to wrong recipient
  • Unauthorized access to electronic records
  • Improper disposal of PHI
  • Discussing patients where others can overhear

Reporting Requirements

  • Report immediately to your privacy officer
  • Document the incident thoroughly
  • Breaches affecting 500+ individuals must be reported to media
  • All breaches must be reported to HHS Office for Civil Rights
Penalties: HIPAA violations can result in fines up to $71,162 per violation (2025 rates) and potential criminal charges.

Prevention Strategies

  • Regular training and awareness programs
  • Implement strong access controls
  • Conduct regular risk assessments
  • Have incident response procedures in place

Patient Rights Under HIPAA

Patients have specific rights regarding their health information that EMS providers must respect.

Key Patient Rights

  • Access: Right to see and get copies of their health records
  • Amendment: Right to request corrections to their health information
  • Accounting: Right to know who has accessed their information
  • Restrictions: Right to request limits on use and disclosure
  • Confidential Communications: Right to request alternative methods of contact
Notice of Privacy Practices: In emergency situations, EMS can provide this notice "as soon as reasonably practicable" after the emergency treatment.

EMS-Specific Considerations

  • Emergency treatment doesn't require prior consent
  • Implied consent applies in life-threatening situations
  • Family members may receive limited information unless patient objects
  • Refer complex patient rights requests to your privacy officer
EMS Scenario: A conscious patient asks you not to transport them to a specific hospital because they don't want their employer to know about their condition. You should respect this request when medically appropriate.
Sources: 45 CFR 164.520; Summary of HIPAA Privacy Rule; Virginia EMS HIPAA guidelines

HIPAA Best Practices for EMS

Key practices to ensure HIPAA compliance in your daily EMS work.

Documentation

  • Document only objective findings
  • Omit personal opinions and non-medical observations
  • Use proper secure disposal methods for paper records
  • Follow NEMSIS standards for electronic documentation

Communication

  • Only discuss patients with authorized personnel
  • Use private areas for patient discussions
  • Be mindful of who can overhear conversations
  • Never give patient information to media without authorization

Technology Security

  • Use strong, unique passwords
  • Enable automatic screen locks
  • Keep software updated with security patches
  • Report security incidents immediately
Remember: When in doubt, protect patient privacy. It's better to be overly cautious than to violate HIPAA requirements.
